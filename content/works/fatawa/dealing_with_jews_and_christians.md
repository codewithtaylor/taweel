# Dealing with Jews and Christians<br>
1 Rabīʿ al-Thānī 1425 AH — corresponding to 20 May 2004

## Question:
What is the meaning of the statement of the Prophet ﷺ: “If you pass by Jews and Christians on the road, force them to its narrowest part”? By analogy, is it permissible to harm them in other matters?

## Answer:
Praise be to Allāh.<br>
What is meant by this ḥadīth—namely the statement of the Prophet ﷺ: “Do not initiate the greeting of peace with Jews and Christians, and if you meet them on the road, force them to its narrowest part”—which is reported by Muslim in his Ṣaḥīḥ from the narration of Abū Hurayrah, is the Jews and Christians who reside in Muslim lands, whether they are from the People of the Covenant (ahl al-dhimmah) or from among those under treaty or granted safe-conduct.

Such people are not to be wronged or assaulted. Nevertheless, it is not permissible to honor them or treat them as Muslims. Thus, one should not initiate the greeting of peace (salām) to them; however, if they greet first, their greeting is to be returned, as the Prophet ﷺ said: “If the People of the Book greet you, then say: ‘And upon you.’” This is reported by al-Bukhārī and Muslim from Anas ibn Mālik (raḍiya Allāhu ʿanhu).

There is no harm in asking them about their condition or some of their affairs. Likewise, one should not make room for them in gatherings as a form of honoring them. If a Muslim encounters one of them on the road, he should not give them the middle of the road; rather, the Muslim proceeds on his way and leaves his side of the road to that disbeliever—whether Jew, Christian, or otherwise. This is the meaning of the Prophet’s ﷺ statement: “If you meet them on the road, force them to its narrowest part.” It is not intended that one compel them to cling to a wall. The instruction in this ḥadīth is not an order to harm them, but an order not to honor them.

On this occasion, we also remind that it is not permissible for a Muslim to use this ḥadīth as a pretext to harass a kāfir while driving—such as by “cutting him off,” as it is said—pressuring him until he is forced off the road, which could lead to harm to him or his vehicle. This is prohibited and constitutes wrongdoing (ẓulm). However, this does not mean honoring him by opening the road for him. As for opening the road to avert danger, this is required for both Muslim and non-Muslim alike; it is a right related to road safety for both.

In summary: wrongdoing (ẓulm) is prohibited, just as honoring a kāfir is prohibited—except for those to whom Allāh has granted a right to kindness, as in His saying:

**“Allāh does not forbid you from those who do not fight you because of religion and do not expel you from your homes—from being righteous toward them and acting justly toward them.”**<br>
(al-Mumtaḥanah 60:8)

Likewise, this applies to parents, as Allāh تعالى says:

**“Be grateful to Me and to your parents; to Me is the final return. But if they strive against you to make you associate with Me that of which you have no knowledge, do not obey them, yet accompany them in this world with kindness, and follow the path of those who turn back to Me.”**<br>
(Luqmān 31:14–15)

Similarly, maintaining ties of kinship (ṣilat al-raḥim) is prescribed whether the relative is Muslim or kāfir.

And Allāh knows best.
